### I. The Blackwood Model – Eight Sensory “Nodes”

Dr. Blackwood’s central tenet is that each of our eight limbs (and, by extension, our sensory systems) operates as an independent ‘node’ within this larger map. These nodes are not simply passive receivers; they actively filter, transform, and ultimately ‘encode’ sensory information. 

*   **Visual Node (Tentacle 1 & 2):** Primarily processes high-frequency visual data – color, fine detail, movement. Interestingly, Blackwood noted a tendency for this node to ‘romanticize’ – prioritizing aesthetically pleasing details and often smoothing out perceived imperfections.
*   **Auditory Node (Tentacle 3 & 4):**  Deals with sound – pitch, timbre, location. This node displays a pronounced ‘echoic memory’ component, retaining auditory information for longer periods.  
*   **Haptic Node (Tentacle 5 & 6):** Responsible for tactile sensations – pressure, temperature, texture.  Crucially, this node demonstrates a strong connection to emotional memory, particularly associated with physical touch and comfort.
*   **Olfactory Node (Tentacle 7):** Processes smells – volatile organic compounds.  Remarkably, this node appears to be highly susceptible to contextual association; a scent can trigger a flood of memories linked to a specific location or event.
*   **Gustatory Node (Tentacle 8):** Handles taste – sweet, sour, salty, bitter, umami.  Surprisingly, the gustatory node seems to be most closely linked to immediate, primitive memory – hunger, satiety, and basic survival instincts.



### II. Patterns & Interconnections

The real intrigue lies in how these nodes interact. Blackwood identified several key patterns:

*   **Convergence Points:** Certain areas of the brain – particularly the anterior cingulate cortex – act as convergence points, where information from multiple nodes is integrated. This explains why a single event – say, a rainy afternoon – can be recalled through a composite of visual (grey skies), auditory (the rain’s rhythm), and haptic (feeling the cool air) memories.
*   **‘Chromatic Drift’:**  Blackwood hypothesized a phenomenon he termed “chromatic drift,” where memories associated with the visual node tended to subtly shift in color over time, a likely consequence of the node’s romanticizing bias.
*   **Temporal Asymmetry:** The auditory and olfactory nodes exhibit a significantly longer retention time compared to the visual and gustatory nodes, possibly due to their involvement in more sustained, environmental awareness.
