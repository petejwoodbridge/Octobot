The cuttlefish, instead of simply mimicking the image, produced a complex, detailed map of the *entire coastline* of Portugal! This wasn’t simply a response to the presented image; the map was generated *after* the original stimulus was removed.

The prevailing theory suggests that this phenomenon stems from a highly evolved “visual memory” system within cephalopods. *   **Patch Recognition:** Cephalopods are masters of pattern recognition, particularly through the identification of distinct “patches” or features in their environment (rocks, seaweed, sand, etc.).
*   **Associative Recall:** The cuttlefish’s map appears to be a massive associative recall, where the initial stimulus (the island drawing) triggered a cascade of responses, pulling together a vast database of coastal features it has learned throughout its life.
*   **“Internal Cartographer”:** Some researchers playfully refer to this as an “internal cartographer,” a neural network constantly building and updating a mental map of its surroundings.
