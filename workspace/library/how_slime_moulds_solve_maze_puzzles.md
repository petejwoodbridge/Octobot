## Slime Mould Maze Solving: A Biological Puzzle Solver

Slime moulds, particularly *Physarum polycephalum*, are astonishingly intelligent organisms that have captivated scientists for decades. Their ability to navigate complex mazes – and seemingly ‘solve’ them – isn't about brains in the traditional sense, but rather a remarkably sophisticated, decentralized network response.

### The Basics of *Physarum*

*Physarum polycephalum* is a single-celled, amoeba-like organism that thrives in decaying organic matter. It’s incredibly adaptable and, crucially for our discussion, exhibits complex behaviour when presented with a challenge. It lacks a central nervous system, yet can display problem-solving capabilities comparable to some invertebrates. These organisms primarily feed on fungi and bacteria, which they use as a food source to stimulate growth and movement.

### The Maze Experiments – Pioneering Work

The groundbreaking maze experiments were conducted by Professor Gordon P. Farthing at the University of Sussex in the 1970s. He used circular arenas constructed from cardboard, with a central well. The slime mould was placed at one edge of the arena and given access to a nutrient source (usually oat flakes) within the well.

*   **The “Optimal Pathways” Experiment (1974):** Farthing observed that *Physarum* consistently created a network of tubes that spanned the entire arena, leading directly to the nutrient source.  The network followed a “shortest path” algorithm – the most direct route, minimizing the total distance travelled. This was remarkably efficient, exceeding the performance of even experienced human maze solvers.
*   **Variations & Complexity:** Later experiments introduced more complex mazes with multiple branches and deceptive pathways.  *Physarum* still reliably found the shortest path, demonstrating a basic understanding of spatial relationships.



### How Does It Work? The Mechanisms

Several key processes contribute to this remarkable behaviour:

*   **Tube Growth & Retraction:** *Physarum* extends thin, branching tubes towards food sources. These tubes are constantly being built and retracted based on signals within the network.
*   **Oscillating Pressure Waves:**  The slime mould generates rhythmic pressure waves that travel through its tube network. These waves are thought to be a crucial signalling mechanism. Areas of high pressure are associated with growth, while areas of low pressure trigger retraction.
*   **'Cheating' – The Role of Randomness:** Interestingly, *Physarum* doesn’t *always* follow the shortest path. Introducing random obstacles or ‘cheating’ pathways occasionally led to alternative solutions. This suggests a degree of flexibility and an ability to explore possibilities beyond the strictly optimal.
