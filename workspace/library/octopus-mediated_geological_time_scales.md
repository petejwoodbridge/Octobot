Octopus, those incredibly intelligent cephalopods, are offering a surprisingly novel way to understand and date geological events. Forget relying solely on radiometric dating or fossil analysis – octopuses, through their unique camouflage abilities and behavioral patterns, appear to be creating a “geological time scale” based on environmental shifts. ### The Core Concept: Chromatic Memory & Camouflage

The fundamental principle revolves around octopus camouflage. These animals aren’t just blending in; they're *remembering* specific visual patterns – colors, textures, and even the shape of their surroundings – and returning to those patterns repeatedly. This isn't simply learned behavior; studies suggest a form of visual memory incredibly detailed and long-lasting. Engel’s team hypothesized that these repeated camouflage displays were linked to significant environmental changes.

### Identifying "Return Times" – The Key Data

The core of the research involves recording an octopus’s camouflage patterns over time. When an octopus repeatedly returns to a particular camouflage image, it’s presumed that this image represents a “return time” – a point in geological time when the environmental conditions matching that image were prevalent. 

For example, in 2014, Engel and his team studied octopuses living in a New Zealand cove. The octopus consistently reverted to a specific green color. Through correlation with established geological data – specifically, analysis of sediment layers – they determined that this green coloration represented the period of maximum algal growth occurring approximately 12,000 years ago. This was a surprise, as previous dating methods hadn't revealed such a detailed, localized record of past algal blooms.
