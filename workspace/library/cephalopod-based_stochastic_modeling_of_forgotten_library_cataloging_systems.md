### Historical Context: The Puzzle of Old Cataloging

Many older library cataloging systems – particularly those developed before the rise of computerized systems – employed idiosyncratic rules and classifications. These weren't simply “bad” systems; they were often built on principles of expert judgment, intuitive categorization, and a deep understanding of the library's collection. However, documentation has often been lost, leaving the *why* behind the categorization shrouded in mystery. Systems like the Cutter system (numeric and alphabetical) or Dewey Decimal (hierarchical) weren’t just random; they stemmed from attempts to impose order on vast, often poorly-defined, collections. The problem is, the rationale has largely vanished.
