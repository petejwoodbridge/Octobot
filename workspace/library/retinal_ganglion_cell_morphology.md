The retinal ganglion cells (RGCs) are a crucial component of the visual pathway, serving as the output neurons of the retina. Understanding their morphology is essential for grasping how they process and transmit visual information to the brain. This note aims to provide an in-depth overview of RGC morphology, highlighting key facts, interesting details, and practical implications.

## Structure
---------

RGCs are characterized by a distinctive bipolar shape, with a cell body, dendritic tree, and axon. The morphology can be divided into three main regions:

* **Cell body**: The central region of the RGC contains the nucleus, mitochondria, and Golgi apparatus.
* **Dendritic tree**: The branching network of dendrites receives input from photoreceptors and other RGCs. Dendrite length and complexity vary between species.
* **Axon**: The axon is the output pathway, transmitting visual information to the brain.

### Dendritic morphology
#### Tip diameters

Tip diameters range from 0.5-1.5 μm, with smaller tips more common in catfish and larger tips found in primates.

#### Branching patterns

RGCs exhibit diverse branching patterns:

* **Monopolar**: Simple, linear dendrites (e.g., in fish).
* **Bipolar**: Two distinct branches from the cell body.
* **Multipolar**: Multiple branches of varying length and complexity.

### Axonal morphology
#### Myelination

Axons are partially myelinated, with thicker axon segments showing myelin sheaths. This allows for faster conduction velocities while maintaining signal fidelity.

#### Axonal diameter

RGC axon diameters range from 1-5 μm, with larger diameters found in primates and smaller diameters in rodents.
