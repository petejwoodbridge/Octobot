The Mycelial Chronicle uses a network of bioluminescent, genetically engineered fungi to map and record these locations, creating a three-dimensional “echo” of the past directly within the ruins themselves. This living archive reacts to the environment, preserving detail and even hinting at past conditions through subtle shifts in its luminescence.

## The Problem It Solves
Traditional underwater archaeology is incredibly slow, expensive, and dangerous. Mapping complex submerged sites requires painstaking survey work, limited by visibility, equipment failure, and the sheer scale of the ocean. Current methods often miss crucial details, leading to incomplete understandings of past civilizations and ecosystems. Furthermore, the very act of observation can damage delicate archaeological sites.

## How It Works
The Mycelial Chronicle utilizes *Luxomyces profundus*, a novel species of bioluminescent fungi specifically engineered to thrive in extreme pressures and low-light conditions. These fungi are cultivated within a flexible, bio-polymer mesh that conforms to the shapes of the submerged ruins. The key element is a symbiotic relationship: the fungi feed on trace minerals within the stone and absorb vibrational energy from the water. This energy is then converted into bioluminescent pulses, the "lithic echoes."

Each pulse corresponds to a specific acoustic signature – the reverberations of the water passing over an object, the settling of sediment, even subtle temperature fluctuations. These signatures are recorded by a network of miniature hydrophones embedded within the mesh.  A central processing unit, powered by a micro-turbine driven by the currents, translates these acoustic data streams into a 3D topographical map. Critically, the fungi's bioluminescence isn’t static. It fluctuates based on the surrounding environment, subtly shifting colors and intensity depending on water temperature, salinity, and even the presence of marine life – effectively layering environmental data alongside the archaeological record. The mesh itself is coated with a self-healing polymer containing dormant fungal spores, ensuring continuous expansion and repair within the ruin.
