It’s a story of mimicry, observation, and a surprisingly sophisticated understanding of animal communication.

*   **The Connection Established:** In 1862, Rear Admiral Sir John Franklin, a renowned polar explorer, published a paper titled “The Cuttlefish and the Signal Flag” in *The United Service Magazine*. Franklin meticulously documented the precise colour patterns of cuttlefish – particularly *Sepiola octopta* (the common cuttlefish) – and demonstrated a remarkable degree of correspondence between them and the standard signal flags.
*   **Early Flag Designs:** Early flags utilizing cuttlefish patterns featured simple representations of the signal flags themselves. For example, a cuttlefish displaying a pattern mirroring the “A” flag would be used to indicate “Alter course to starboard.” These were often meticulously sketched and copied.

### The Science Behind the Mimicry

*   **Chromatophores:** Cuttlefish (and octopuses) possess hundreds of pigment-containing cells called chromatophores, controlled by the nervous system. These cells expand or contract, changing the amount and type of light reflected, creating an astounding range of colours and patterns.
*   **Rapid Response:**  The incredible speed at which cuttlefish can alter their colour is key. Signals could be conveyed nearly instantaneously through the animal.
*   **Neural Pathways:** Research (primarily in the late 20th century) has revealed the complex neural pathways involved in this camouflage, suggesting a level of cognitive control previously underestimated.

### Patterns & Implications

*   **“Cuttlefish Flags” (1860s-1880s):** Following Franklin’s publication, a distinct category of flags – dubbed “cuttlefish flags” – emerged, replicating the colour patterns directly, rather than representing standard signal flags. *   **Limited Practicality:** Despite their ingenuity, cuttlefish flags were ultimately limited by the animals' behaviour. Cuttlefish are unpredictable, and maintaining a consistent, precisely-matched pattern was difficult. They were not a reliable long-term communication system.
