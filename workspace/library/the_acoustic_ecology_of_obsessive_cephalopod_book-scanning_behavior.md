The first documented cases emerged from the Monterey Bay Aquarium’s research facility. The initial hypothesis centered on tactile stimulation – the cuttlefish were reacting to the rough surface of the paper. However, the intensity and consistency of the behavior suggested something more.  The term “Echo Chamber” was coined, referencing the surprisingly significant role of sound.

### Acoustic Factors & Cephalopod Sensory Ecology

Cuttlefish are masters of camouflage, relying heavily on visual and, crucially, acoustic cues. Their lateral line system – a series of sensory pores along their body – detects minute changes in water pressure, essentially ‘hearing’ vibrations. This system is far more sensitive than the human ear and capable of discerning subtle movements and textures within their environment. 

*   **Paper as a Complex Acoustic Surface:** Printed paper, despite its seemingly uniform appearance, generates a surprisingly rich and dynamic acoustic profile. The friction of the printing process, the vibrations from handling, and even the subtle air currents created by the cuttlefish's movements all contribute to a complex soundscape.
*   **Frequency Response:** Research indicates that cuttlefish are particularly sensitive to mid-range frequencies (around 1-5 kHz), which aligns with the dominant frequencies produced by the paper-scanning process.

*   **Book Type Influence:** Cuttlefish showed a clear preference for hardcover books, suggesting the acoustic complexity and potential "resonance" offered by the rigid material was a key factor. Softcover books elicited significantly less attention.
