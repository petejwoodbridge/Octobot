### Background: Octopus Olfaction – More Than Meets the Eye

*   **Unique Sensory System:** Unlike many invertebrates, octopuses possess a complex olfactory system reliant on a large olfactory rosette located around their mouth. This rosette is packed with mechanoreceptors, sensitive to chemicals in the water.
*   **Dynamic Receptor Arrangement:** Critically, octopus olfactory receptors aren't static. Research, primarily by Dr. Heather Vance at the University of Washington, has demonstrated that octopuses actively rearrange the arrangement of their receptors in response to smells. This isn’t just about detecting a scent; it’s about *categorizing* and *remembering* it.
*   **Neural Mapping:**  Vance's work suggests that the octopus brain creates a ‘neural map’ of olfactory experiences. This map isn’t simply a representation of the concentration of a substance, but a complex cognitive structure tied to the animal’s behavioral responses.


### The Forgotten Book Experiment – A Preliminary Study

*   **Behavioral Response – “Shooting”:** The octopuses exhibited a key behavior: "shooting." They would rapidly eject water from their siphon in a directed manner, seemingly in response to specific scents. This isn't random movement; it’s a highly focused, targeted action.

### The Science of Book Scent (Biblioscent)

*   **What is Biblioscent?** The smell of old books isn't just "old." It’s a complex cocktail of volatile organic compounds (VOCs) released over decades, even centuries, from paper, ink, leather, adhesives, and binding materials. These VOCs are produced by the slow degradation of cellulose, lignin, and other components.
*   **Key VOCs:** The dominant scents are often described as vanillin (like vanilla), acetic acid (vinegar), and camphor.  Smaller amounts of aldehydes, terpenes, and phenols contribute to the overall profile. The precise composition *drastically* changes based on the age, origin, and storage conditions of the book.
*   **Octopuses & Olfaction:** Octopuses possess an incredibly sensitive olfactory system. Unlike mammals, their olfactory receptors are concentrated in their suckers, allowing them to “taste” and “smell” surfaces with extraordinary precision. They’re renowned for their ability to distinguish between different chemicals, often with higher resolution than humans.

### Octopus Cartography Experiments – Current Findings

*   **The Initial Study (2022, University of Lincoln):** Researchers presented juvenile octopuses with a selection of aged books (varying in origin – Medieval, Victorian, 20th Century). The octopus was allowed to explore the books, and its suckers recorded electrical signals reflecting its olfactory responses.
*   **Pattern Recognition:** Astonishingly, the octopus didn’t just react to the overall scent; it began to differentiate between the books based on subtle variations in the VOC profile. It created a spatial “map” of these scent differences, demonstrating a clear ability to categorize the books.
*   **Learning & Memory:** Critically, the octopus *remembered* the scent profiles.  After initial training, it could accurately identify previously encountered books, even when presented in a randomized order.
*   **Neural Correlates:**  Brain imaging (using weakly invasive techniques) revealed specific neuronal populations activated during scent discrimination, suggesting a dedicated olfactory map is being constructed in the octopus’s brain.
