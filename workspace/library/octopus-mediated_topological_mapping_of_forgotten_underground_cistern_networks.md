For centuries, many Mediterranean and Middle Eastern civilizations (Romans, Egyptians, Nabataeans, etc.) relied on sophisticated cistern systems to collect, store, and distribute rainwater. These networks, crucial for survival in arid climates, have largely vanished, often due to subsidence, looting, or simply being swallowed by the earth. Traditional methods of mapping – surveying, geophysical scanning – can be slow, expensive, and prone to inaccuracies, especially when dealing with complex, interconnected subterranean spaces.

### Enter the Octopus: A Natural Navigator

The core of this research revolves around the incredible navigational abilities of octopuses. These invertebrates possess an extraordinary “grid cell” system in their brains. This system doesn't represent space as a flat map; instead, it generates a 3D representation of their environment, constantly updating as the octopus explores.  Crucially, the system seems to be *topologically* aware – it understands the connections and relationships between spaces, even if the individual spaces are slightly different.
