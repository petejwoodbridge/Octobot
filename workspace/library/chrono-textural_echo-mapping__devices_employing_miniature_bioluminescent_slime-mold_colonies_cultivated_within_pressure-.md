## How It Works
The device centers around a 3D-printed ceramic chamber, roughly the size of a palm, fabricated with micro-channels and embedded pressure sensors. Within this chamber, a carefully selected strain of bioluminescent *Physarum polycephalum* (slime mold) is cultivated. The stone surface being “read” is mounted onto a small, articulated arm that gently presses against the ceramic chamber.  

Here’s the key: The ceramic chamber isn’t just a passive recipient; it’s an active sensor. Miniature, piezoelectric sensors within the chamber detect minute vibrations and thermal fluctuations transmitted from the stone through physical contact. This data is then fed into a micro-controller. The controller, programmed with algorithms mimicking geological processes – erosion patterns, thermal cycles, seismic events – dictates the nutrient supply to the slime mold. 

The slime mold responds by growing, retracting, and shifting color intensity based on the received data.  Areas of high stress or repeated thermal changes will trigger faster, denser growth and a brighter, almost neon-blue bioluminescence. Conversely, stable periods will cause slower, more delicate growth and a softer, amber glow. The pressure sensors also influence the mold’s growth patterns, creating topographical features reflecting the stone’s surface.  Real-time data is streamed wirelessly to a connected tablet, allowing the user to record and analyze these “echo-maps” – creating a digital archive of the stone’s past. The entire system is self-regulating; the slime mold’s metabolic activity is tied directly to the input data, ensuring constant evolution of the echo-map.
