### Background: Cephalopod Circadian Rhythms – A Delicate Dance

* **Robust Circadian Clocks:** Cephalopods are renowned for having exceptionally robust circadian clocks. These clocks aren’t just about sleep-wake cycles; they govern a huge range of physiological processes – feeding, camouflage, jet lag avoidance (they migrate across vast distances!), and even immune responses.
* **Molecular Mechanisms:** The primary circadian clock in cephalopods relies on a core set of genes: *per*, *pmk*, *cry*, and *clock*.  Similar to mammals, these genes produce proteins that oscillate in concentration, driving the cycle. However, cephalopod clocks appear to be particularly sensitive to external cues like light-dark cycles.
* **Light Sensitivity:** Cephalopods are intensely phototactic – drawn to light. They use specialized photoreceptors in their skin for this, and their clock is powerfully influenced by light exposure.  This sensitivity makes them ideal candidates for observing rhythm-related changes in response to environmental manipulation.


### Cellulose Decay & the Role of Biological Activity

* **Microbial Involvement:** Cellulose degradation is primarily driven by microorganisms (bacteria, fungi) that thrive in humid environments. These organisms produce enzymes that break down cellulose chains.
* **Humidity as a Key Factor:** Humidity levels are *critical*. Higher humidity fuels microbial activity and accelerates decay.
* **Biological Activity – A Surprising Link:** Recent research (primarily led by Dr. Stephanie Wright at the University of Lincoln) has suggested that *biological activity* – specifically, the presence of a living organism – can accelerate cellulose degradation rates. * **Initial Findings:** Experiments involving exposing paper samples to cephalopod tissue (specifically, octopus tissue) demonstrated a statistically significant acceleration in cellulose decay compared to control samples. The rate of decay was most pronounced within the period of the cephalopod’s circadian rhythm.
