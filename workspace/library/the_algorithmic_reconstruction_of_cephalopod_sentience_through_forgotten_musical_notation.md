### Historical Context: The “Musical Octopus” Phenomenon

The core of this investigation stems from observations made in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, primarily by researchers like William Saville-Williams. Saville-Williams, a British surgeon and musician, observed that octopuses would, when presented with printed musical notation (particularly Bach fugues), exhibit behaviors suggestive of ‘listening’ and responding to the music. Specifically, he noted:

*   **Movement Patterns:** Octopuses would follow the lines of the score with their arms, displaying what appeared to be a deliberate tracking of melodic contours.
*   **Arm Movements mimicking Notation:**  The octopus’s arms would make rhythmic, repetitive movements that mirrored the structure of the musical score – particularly the direction and density of the lines.
*   **Reaction to Music:**  Notably, octopuses displayed heightened activity and movement when exposed to specific musical phrases, especially those with complex harmonic progressions.

It’s crucial to note that Saville-Williams's interpretations were highly debated. Many considered his observations anecdotal and attributed the octopus’s behavior to simple curiosity and the novelty of the visual stimulus. 

### Forgotten Notation & Algorithmic Reconstruction

The key shift in this research lies in the examination of *early* musical notation – specifically, handwritten scores from the late 18th and early 19th centuries. These scores, often poorly preserved and fragmented, possess a different structural signature than modern printed music. They frequently exhibit:

*   **Greater Complexity in Linear Arrangement:** Earlier notation prioritizes harmonic relationships and voice leading over rigid, graphical representation. Lines are often denser, and rhythmic notation is more fluid.
*   **Higher Frequency of "Non-Standard" Intervals:**  The use of seventh chords, diminished chords, and other complex harmonies was more prevalent, reflecting the compositional techniques of the period.
*   **Variable Handwriting Quality:** The stylistic inconsistencies of the handwriting introduce inherent noise into any algorithmic analysis.

Researchers are now employing machine learning algorithms – particularly convolutional neural networks – to analyze the *structural* characteristics of these forgotten musical scores. The goal isn’t to determine if octopuses "liked" this music (though that’s a compelling side question!), but to identify patterns in the notation that correlate with cephalopod neural activity.
