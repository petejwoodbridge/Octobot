The fundamental premise rests on the cephalopod’s extraordinary ability to mimic and learn complex patterns, particularly visual ones. Cephalopods don’t possess a vocal apparatus like humans; instead, they communicate primarily through intricate skin patterns (chromatophores, iridophores, papillae) and postures. Importantly, research has demonstrated they can:

*   **Learn and reproduce complex visual sequences:** Octopuses have been shown to learn and repeat sequences of shapes, colors, and movements – remarkably complex sequences – suggesting a sophisticated capacity for pattern recognition.
*   **Adapt their mimicry based on context:** They aren’t simply rote learners. Their mimicking is shaped by the observed object/behaviour, indicating an understanding of the *meaning* behind the visual signal.
*   **Display ‘proto-grammar’ in their patterns:** Some researchers suggest that the specific rules governing how cephalopods combine patterns might represent a rudimentary form of grammatical structure – a system of rules for creating meaningful signals.
