### The Core Concept: Sediment as a Memory Bank

The fundamental premise is that human activity – particularly ritualistic practices, offerings, or settlements – associated with cephalopod myths would leave behind traces in the sediment layer. These traces aren’t just organic matter; they include:

*   **Metal Isotopes:** Ancient cultures often used metals (copper, tin, gold) in religious artifacts and offerings. The deposition of these metals, influenced by rainfall patterns, currents, and human activity, creates a unique isotopic fingerprint.
*   **Pigment Distributions:**  Octopuses and squid possess intensely colored pigments.  If these were used in rituals or as part of offerings, the breakdown of these pigments would leave a detectable trace in the sediment. The spectral analysis of these traces can then be matched against known pigment compositions.
*   **Microbial Communities:**  The sediment harbours microbial communities. The composition of these communities can shift dramatically based on the presence of organic material (from offerings, for example) and therefore provide a bio-signature related to past human activity.

*   **Raman Spectroscopy:** To identify the breakdown products of cephalopod pigments – particularly blues and greens – within the sediment.
