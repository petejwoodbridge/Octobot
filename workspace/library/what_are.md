### 1. The Linguistic Foundation: Semantics and Definition

*   **The Question Itself:** The question isn’t just about the existence of something; it’s about *categorizing* that existence. It prompts a response that clarifies a thing’s identity.

### 2. Philosophical Perspectives – Being and Categorization

*   **Plato’s Forms:** Plato’s Theory of Forms posits that objects in the physical world are merely imperfect reflections of eternal, perfect Forms.  "What are" questions force us to grapple with the underlying Form versus the observable instance.  Is a table *really* a table, or are we perceiving a manifestation of the abstract "Table-ness"?
*   **Aristotle’s Categorization:** Aristotle proposed a system of categorization based on shared characteristics (genus and differentia). “What are” becomes a tool for applying this system, seeking to place something within a defined category (e.g., “What are you?” – attempting to categorize a person based on their profession, age, etc.).
*   **Existentialism:**  Existentialist thinkers challenge the notion of inherent definitions. They argue that “what are” questions are inherently problematic, as human existence is defined by our choices and experiences, not pre-determined categories.



### 3. Scientific & Operational Definitions

*   **Scientific Definitions:** In science, “what are” questions drive the process of defining variables and concepts within a specific context.  For example, "What are the properties of dark matter?" – a scientific definition seeks measurable characteristics.
*   **Operational Definitions:**  Often, particularly in psychology or medicine, a concept needs an *operational* definition – a description of how it will be measured or observed. "What are symptoms of depression?" relies on a set of observable behaviors and self-reported feelings.
*   **Pattern: Contextual Dependence:** The "correct" answer to "What are?" *always* depends on the surrounding context. A definition for a medical term will differ greatly from a definition of a literary device.
