## The Problem It Solves
Traditional archaeological interpretation relies heavily on visual evidence and textual analysis, leaving a significant gap in our understanding of daily life and the environment in which ancient tools were used. We can examine a tool, but we can’t truly *know* what it smelled like – the scents of the craftsperson’s workshop, the surrounding landscape, or even the materials used to create the tool. Current methods of recreating ancient environments are largely inaccurate and lack the nuance of a truly sensory reconstruction.

## How It Works
EchoStone utilizes a sophisticated combination of technologies. First, a handheld device captures the vibrational signature of an ancient stone tool using high-resolution spectroscopy, analyzing its material composition and structural nuances. Piezoelectric sensors then translate these vibrations into electrical signals, which are fed into a miniature, highly controlled bio-reactor. This reactor contains a curated library of organic compounds – resins, oils, pigments, and plant extracts – each selected for its historical relevance to toolmaking and the environments where those tools were used. The bio-reactor uses the vibrational data as an algorithmic key, synthesizing volatile organic compounds (VOCs) that best match the tool’s “scent fingerprint”. 

Simultaneously, the user wears a haptic suit constructed from a flexible, sensor-embedded material. This suit subtly mimics the texture and weight of the original stone tool, providing a tactile connection to the artifact. Finally, the device displays a 3D holographic projection of the tool, overlaid with a dynamic scent cloud representing the reconstructed historical context – a constantly shifting aroma reflecting the sensory environment.
