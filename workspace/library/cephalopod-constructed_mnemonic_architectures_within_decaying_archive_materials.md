### Observed Patterns & Archival Anomalies

Several researchers (primarily independent, small-scale investigations) have reported unusual patterns in highly degraded archival collections:

*   **‘Ink Veins’:**  The most common observation is the appearance of branching, network-like patterns resembling veins or roots within parchment or paper. These are frequently darkest in areas of greatest decay.
*   **Geometric Forms:**  Occasionally, more complex geometric shapes – spirals, circles, and even rudimentary representations of marine life – have been identified within these ‘ink veins.’ The regularity of these forms is perplexing.
*   **Localized Concentration:** Patterns tend to cluster around areas of significant water damage or microbial activity, suggesting the ink’s response to environmental stressors.
*   **Case Study: The Alistair Blackwood Collection:**  A small collection of 18th-century maritime journals exhibited particularly pronounced ‘ink veins.’ Analysis revealed a remarkably high concentration of *Penicillium* species – common in damp archival environments – alongside the cephalopod ink.
